About Track & Tide

Track & Tide is an interactive map of every train station and ferry port in Europe. It helps you discover which operators serve a station, explore ferry routes between ports, and see live departure times — all in one place.

How it started

My name is Boas, and I am a solo developer with a deep love for train travel. There is something about standing on a platform somewhere in Europe, looking at a departure board, and knowing you can be in another country within hours — no airports, no check-ins, just rails.

I built Track & Tide because I wanted a single map where I could see all European train stations and ferry routes at once. Existing tools either focus on one country, one operator, or are overloaded with ads and booking links. I wanted something cleaner: just the map, the stations, the operators, and the routes. No noise.

The name comes from the two things that move people across Europe: tracks on land and tides at sea. Whether you are taking an Intercity across Germany or a ferry from Barcelona to Palma, Track & Tide has you covered.

Where the data comes from

Wikidata

Station and port information is sourced from Wikidata, the free knowledge graph behind Wikipedia. Wikidata contains structured data about millions of places, including railway stations and ferry terminals — their coordinates, operators, number of tracks, passenger statistics, and even photos. Every station page on Track & Tide links back to its Wikidata entry so you can explore the raw data or contribute corrections.

OpenStreetMap

For stations and ports not yet in Wikidata, we pull coordinates and names from OpenStreetMap, the community-maintained map of the world. OSM is updated daily by thousands of volunteers, making it the most comprehensive geographic dataset available. If you notice a missing station or incorrect location, you can fix it directly on OpenStreetMap and the change will appear on Track & Tide.

Live departures

Real-time departure data is powered by a self-hosted MOTIS server, providing transit schedules for the Netherlands. When you click on a station, you see actual upcoming trains and their real-time delay information — no proprietary data, no commercial feeds, just open data at work.

Operators

The operator database is manually curated. Each railway and ferry company — from Deutsche Bahn to Greenland Ferry Lines — has been researched, verified, and linked to the correct stations and routes. This is the most labour-intensive part of the project, and also what makes Track & Tide unique: knowing who runs the trains at each station, not just where the station is.

Why open data matters

Everything on Track & Tide comes from open, freely-licensed sources. This means the data is transparent, verifiable, and anyone can contribute improvements. If you find a mistake — a wrong operator, a missing route, an outdated station name — you can fix it at the source (Wikidata or OpenStreetMap) and the correction will flow through to Track & Tide automatically. No walled gardens, no proprietary silos.

This also means Track & Tide will never charge for access or hide data behind a paywall. The trains belong to everyone. So should the map.

Built by one person

Track & Tide is developed and maintained by Boas, a solo developer based in the Netherlands. There is no team, no funding, no startup behind this — just someone who really likes trains, maps, and open data. The project runs on a single server, costs almost nothing to operate, and is built with plain HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and a handful of open-source tools (MapLibre, Node.js, etc).

If you enjoy using Track & Tide, the best way to support it is to contribute to OpenStreetMap or improve station data on Wikidata. Every edit makes the map better for everyone.
